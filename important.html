üßæ Adult Fainting Emergency: First Aid for Syncope

If an adult faints or feels faint, act quickly to ensure their safety. Help them lie down on their back and elevate their legs about 12 inches to improve blood flow to the brain. Keep the person lying down until they feel better and are ready to sit up slowly. If they do not regain consciousness within a minute, or if the fainting is accompanied by other symptoms like chest pain or difficulty breathing, seek emergency medical help immediately.

‚ö†Ô∏è Recognizing Fainting or Feeling Faint

  • Sudden dizziness or lightheadedness
  • Weakness or nausea
  • Blurred or tunnel vision
  • Pale, cold, or clammy skin
  • Loss of consciousness or brief blackout
  • Confusion or disorientation after fainting
  • Rapid or weak pulse
  • Shallow or irregular breathing

üöë What to Do if Someone Faints

  • Help the person lie down on their back immediately
  • Elevate their legs about 12 inches (30 cm) to increase blood flow to the brain
  • Loosen any tight clothing around the neck or waist
  • Ensure fresh air and keep the area calm
  • Check responsiveness and breathing regularly
  • If unconscious for more than a minute or if seizures, chest pain, or difficulty breathing occur, call emergency services

üßç‚Äç‚ôÇÔ∏è Helping the Person Recover

  • Keep the person lying down until they feel fully recovered
  • Encourage slow, deep breaths
  • When ready, help them sit up slowly, then stand with support
  • Avoid sudden movements to prevent repeat fainting
  • Offer water if the person is alert and able to drink

üîÑ Continue Monitoring Until Fully Recovered

  • Watch for changes in consciousness or breathing
  • Do not leave the person alone until fully alert
  • Seek medical advice if fainting recurs or if underlying health issues are suspected
  • Call emergency services if the person does not regain consciousness promptly

üÜò When to Seek Emergency Help

  • Loss of consciousness lasts longer than 1 minute
  • Seizures or convulsions occur
  • Chest pain, difficulty breathing, or severe headache
  • Injury caused by the fall
  • Repeated fainting episodes
  • Known heart conditions or other serious health issues

üßæ Adult First Aid: Caring for Minor Cuts and Scrapes

If someone has a minor cut or scrape, stay calm and provide prompt care to prevent infection and promote healing. Clean the wound gently with water to remove dirt and debris. Apply pressure to stop any bleeding. Cover the wound with a sterile bandage or dressing. Avoid using harsh chemicals or scrubbing the wound. Seek medical help if the cut is deep, won‚Äôt stop bleeding, or shows signs of infection.

‚ö†Ô∏è Recognizing Minor Cuts and Scrapes

  • Small, shallow breaks in the skin
  • Light bleeding or oozing
  • Redness and mild swelling around the wound
  • Possible dirt, gravel, or debris in the wound
  • Pain or tenderness at the injury site
  • No signs of deep tissue damage or severe bleeding

üöë What to Do for Minor Cuts and Scrapes

  • Wash your hands thoroughly before touching the wound
  • Rinse the wound gently with clean water to remove dirt and debris
  • Use mild soap around the wound if needed, but avoid getting soap inside
  • Apply gentle pressure with a clean cloth or gauze to stop bleeding
  • Once bleeding stops, apply an antibiotic ointment to prevent infection
  • Cover the wound with a sterile adhesive bandage or dressing

üßç‚Äç‚ôÇÔ∏è Proper Wound Care

  • Keep the wound clean and dry
  • Change the bandage daily or whenever it becomes wet or dirty
  • Avoid picking at scabs to promote healing
  • Watch for signs of infection such as increased redness, swelling, or pus
  • If the wound is painful or not healing properly, consult a healthcare professional

üîÑ Continue Care Until the Wound Heals

  • Keep the wound covered and clean during the healing process
  • Protect the area from further injury or irritation
  • Encourage the person to rest if the injury causes discomfort
  • Seek medical advice if redness, swelling, or pain worsens

üÜò When to Seek Emergency Help

  • Bleeding does not stop after applying pressure for 10 minutes
  • The cut is deep, gaping, or caused by a dirty or rusty object
  • Signs of infection develop, such as redness, warmth, swelling, or pus
  • The person has not had a tetanus shot in the last 5 years
  • There is numbness, severe pain, or loss of function near the injury
  • Debris cannot be removed easily or wound edges cannot be closed

üßæ Adult First Aid: Caring for Minor Burns

If an adult suffers a minor burn, act quickly and calmly. Cool the burn with running cool (not cold) water for at least 10 minutes to reduce pain and swelling. Avoid using ice or very cold water, which can damage the tissue. Cover the burn with a clean, non-stick dressing. Do not apply creams, ointments, or greasy substances. Seek medical help if the burn is larger than 3 inches, or shows signs of infection.

‚ö†Ô∏è Recognizing Minor Burns

  • Redness and mild swelling of the skin
  • Pain or tenderness at the burn site
  • Blisters that may be small and intact
  • Skin feels warm to the touch
  • No deep tissue damage or charred skin
  • Burn affects only the outer layers of the skin

üöë What to Do for Minor Burns

  • Remove the person from the source of the burn
  • Cool the burn immediately with running cool water for 10-20 minutes
  • Do not use ice or very cold water on the burn
  • Remove tight clothing or jewelry near the burn area unless stuck
  • Cover the burn with a sterile, non-stick dressing or clean cloth
  • Do not apply butter, oils, creams, or adhesive bandages directly on the burn

üßç‚Äç‚ôÇÔ∏è Proper Burn Care

  • Keep the burn clean and dry once cooled
  • Change the dressing daily or if it becomes wet or dirty
  • Avoid popping any blisters that form
  • Use over-the-counter pain relief if needed
  • Watch for signs of infection such as increased redness, swelling, or pus
  • Seek medical advice if healing is delayed or complications arise

üîÑ Continue Care Until the Burn Heals

  • Keep the area protected from friction or further injury
  • Maintain good hygiene to prevent infection
  • Avoid exposing the burn to direct sunlight during healing
  • Follow up with a healthcare provider if the burn worsens or does not improve

üÜò When to Seek Emergency Help

  • Burn covers a large area (more than 3 inches or a palm-sized area)
  • Burn affects face, hands, feet, genitals, or major joints
  • Burn is deep, white, charred, or causes numbness
  • Signs of infection such as increased pain, redness, swelling, or pus
  • Person shows signs of shock (pale, clammy skin, rapid breathing, weakness)
  • Burn was caused by chemicals, electricity, or inhalation injury

üßæ Adult Seizure Emergency: First Aid for Seizures

If an adult is having a seizure, stay calm and protect them from injury. Clear the area around them and gently guide them to the floor if possible. Do not restrain their movements or put anything in their mouth. Time the seizure and stay with the person until it ends. Seek emergency medical help if the seizure lasts longer than 5 minutes, or if it is their first seizure.

‚ö†Ô∏è Recognizing a Seizure

  • Loss of consciousness or awareness
  • Uncontrollable jerking or convulsions of arms and legs
  • Stiffening of the body
  • Drooling, frothing at the mouth, or biting the tongue
  • Confusion or unresponsiveness after convulsions
  • Sudden staring or blanking out (absence seizure)
  • Loss of muscle control, causing the person to collapse

üöë What to Do if Someone Has a Seizure

  • Protect the person from injury by moving sharp or hard objects away
  • Gently guide them to the floor and place something soft under their head
  • Turn the person onto their side to keep the airway clear and prevent choking
  • Do not hold them down or try to stop their movements
  • Do not put anything in their mouth, including fingers or objects
  • Time the seizure duration and stay with them until it ends

üßç‚Äç‚ôÇÔ∏è After the Seizure Ends

  • Check the person‚Äôs breathing and responsiveness
  • Keep them lying on their side in the recovery position
  • Provide reassurance and comfort as they regain awareness
  • Allow them to rest as they may feel tired or confused
  • Do not offer food or drink until fully alert

üîÑ When to Call Emergency Services

  • Seizure lasts longer than 5 minutes
  • Person has repeated seizures without regaining consciousness
  • Seizure happens in water
  • Person is injured, pregnant, or has diabetes
  • This is the person‚Äôs first seizure
  • Difficulty breathing or waking up after the seizure

üßæ Adult First Aid: Caring for Allergic Reactions

If an adult shows signs of an allergic reaction, act quickly and calmly. Identify and remove the allergen if possible. Mild reactions can be managed by antihistamines, but watch carefully for signs of severe reactions (anaphylaxis). If symptoms worsen, including difficulty breathing or swelling of the face, seek emergency medical help immediately. Use an epinephrine auto-injector if available and trained to do so.

‚ö†Ô∏è Recognizing an Allergic Reaction

  • Itching, redness, or rash on the skin
  • Hives (raised, itchy bumps)
  • Swelling of the lips, face, tongue, or throat
  • Difficulty breathing, wheezing, or coughing
  • Runny nose, sneezing, or watery eyes
  • Abdominal pain, nausea, or vomiting
  • Dizziness, fainting, or rapid heartbeat (signs of severe reaction)

üöë What to Do for an Allergic Reaction

  • Help the person sit or lie down comfortably
  • Remove the allergen if known and safely possible
  • Administer antihistamines for mild symptoms if available
  • Use an epinephrine auto-injector immediately if severe symptoms occur
  • Loosen tight clothing and help them breathe slowly and calmly
  • Call emergency services immediately if symptoms worsen or anaphylaxis is suspected

üßç‚Äç‚ôÇÔ∏è Aftercare and Monitoring

  • Stay with the person and monitor their condition closely
  • If epinephrine was given, seek emergency medical help even if symptoms improve
  • Encourage the person to avoid known allergens in the future
  • Record the time and details of the reaction and treatment
  • Be prepared to assist with a second dose of epinephrine if symptoms return

üîÑ Continue Care Until Help Arrives

  • Keep the person calm and reassured
  • Ensure their airway remains open and breathing steady
  • Position them on their side if they feel faint or start vomiting
  • Do not give food or drink if they have difficulty swallowing

üÜò When to Seek Emergency Help

  • Difficulty breathing, wheezing, or throat swelling
  • Loss of consciousness or confusion
  • Rapid or weak pulse, dizziness, or fainting
  • Swelling spreading rapidly or severe rash
  • Repeated vomiting or severe abdominal pain
  • Any suspected anaphylaxis or severe allergic reaction

üßæ Adult First Aid: Caring for an Asthma Attack

If an adult is having an asthma attack, stay calm and help them sit upright comfortably. Encourage slow, deep breaths and assist them to use their prescribed inhaler immediately. If symptoms do not improve or worsen after treatment, seek emergency medical help without delay.

‚ö†Ô∏è Recognizing an Asthma Attack

  • Difficulty breathing or shortness of breath
  • Wheezing or whistling sound when exhaling
  • Chest tightness or pain
  • Coughing, especially at night or early morning
  • Difficulty speaking full sentences
  • Feeling anxious or panic due to breathlessness
  • Blue lips or fingernails (sign of severe attack)

üöë What to Do During an Asthma Attack

  • Help the person sit upright and stay calm
  • Assist them to use their reliever inhaler (usually blue) as prescribed
  • Encourage slow, deep breaths to help control breathing
  • Wait 4 minutes and if symptoms persist, repeat inhaler dose
  • If no improvement after 2 doses, call emergency services immediately
  • Do not leave the person alone while waiting for help

üßç‚Äç‚ôÇÔ∏è Supporting the Person

  • Encourage calm and reassurance to reduce anxiety
  • Loosen tight clothing around the neck and chest
  • Keep the environment free from smoke, dust, or allergens
  • If prescribed, help them use a spacer device with the inhaler
  • Monitor for worsening symptoms or loss of consciousness

üîÑ Continue Care Until Help Arrives

  • Keep the person sitting upright and breathing steadily
  • Stay with them and offer reassurance
  • Do not give them anything to eat or drink if they are struggling to breathe
  • Be ready to perform CPR if the person becomes unresponsive

üÜò When to Seek Emergency Help

  • Symptoms worsen or do not improve after using inhaler
  • Difficulty speaking or inability to complete sentences
  • Blue or grey lips, face, or nails
  • Extreme difficulty breathing or gasping for air
  • Loss of consciousness or confusion
  • No improvement after two inhaler doses

üßæ Adult First Aid: Caring for Splinters & Small Foreign Objects

If an adult has a splinter or small foreign object embedded in the skin, act calmly and carefully. Clean the area gently with soap and water, and try to remove the object with clean tweezers if it is easily accessible. Avoid digging too deep or forcing removal. Cover the area with a sterile bandage and watch for signs of infection. Seek medical attention if the object is deeply embedded, causes severe pain, or shows signs of infection.

‚ö†Ô∏è Recognizing Splinters and Small Foreign Objects

  • Small sharp object lodged under the skin (wood, glass, metal, etc.)
  • Localized pain, tenderness, or discomfort
  • Redness and mild swelling around the area
  • Possible bleeding or oozing from the wound
  • Difficulty moving nearby fingers or toes if on hands or feet
  • Signs of infection such as increasing redness, warmth, pus, or fever

üöë What to Do for Splinters and Small Foreign Objects

  • Wash your hands thoroughly before assisting
  • Clean the affected area gently with soap and water
  • Use sterilized tweezers to carefully remove the splinter if visible and easily accessible
  • Avoid digging deeply or forcing the object out
  • If the object is deeply embedded, seek professional medical help
  • Apply an antiseptic after removal and cover with a sterile dressing

üßç‚Äç‚ôÇÔ∏è Proper Care After Removal

  • Keep the wound clean and dry
  • Change the dressing daily or if it becomes wet or dirty
  • Watch for signs of infection such as redness, swelling, pus, or increasing pain
  • Avoid scratching or irritating the wound
  • Use over-the-counter pain relief if needed
  • Seek medical advice if healing is slow or complications arise

üîÑ Continue Care Until the Wound Heals

  • Protect the area from dirt and friction
  • Maintain good hygiene to prevent infection
  • Avoid soaking the wound for prolonged periods
  • Follow up with a healthcare provider if redness, swelling, or pain worsens

üÜò When to Seek Emergency Help

  • Object is deeply embedded or large
  • Excessive bleeding or inability to stop bleeding
  • Signs of infection such as increasing redness, warmth, swelling, or pus
  • Severe pain or loss of sensation near the wound
  • Signs of tetanus infection (muscle stiffness, difficulty swallowing)
  • If the person has not had a tetanus shot in the last 5 years

üßæ Adult First Aid: Caring for Heat Exhaustion

If an adult shows signs of heat exhaustion, act quickly and calmly. Move the person to a cooler, shaded area and help them lie down. Loosen or remove tight clothing and encourage slow, deep breathing. Offer cool (not cold) water to drink if they are conscious and able to swallow. Use cool compresses or a fan to lower their body temperature. Seek medical help if symptoms worsen or do not improve within 30 minutes.

‚ö†Ô∏è Recognizing Heat Exhaustion

  • Heavy sweating and clammy, pale skin
  • Weakness or fatigue
  • Dizziness or fainting
  • Headache and muscle cramps
  • Nausea or vomiting
  • Rapid, shallow breathing and fast heartbeat
  • Feeling very thirsty and dry mouth

üöë What to Do for Heat Exhaustion

  • Move the person to a cool, shaded, or air-conditioned place
  • Help them lie down and elevate their legs slightly
  • Loosen or remove tight or heavy clothing
  • Encourage sipping cool water slowly (if conscious and able to drink)
  • Apply cool, damp cloths to the skin or use a fan to help cool down
  • Avoid giving alcohol or caffeine

üßç‚Äç‚ôÇÔ∏è Proper Care After Initial Treatment

  • Monitor the person closely for worsening symptoms
  • Encourage rest in a cool environment
  • Continue to offer fluids if tolerated
  • Watch for signs of heat stroke such as confusion, loss of consciousness, or high body temperature
  • Seek medical advice if symptoms do not improve within 30 minutes

üîÑ Continue Care Until Fully Recovered

  • Avoid strenuous activity until fully recovered
  • Maintain hydration with water or oral rehydration solutions
  • Wear light, breathable clothing in hot weather
  • Stay in cool or air-conditioned environments
  • Follow up with a healthcare provider if symptoms recur or worsen

üÜò When to Seek Emergency Help

  • Person loses consciousness or faints
  • Symptoms worsen or do not improve after 30 minutes of care
  • Signs of heat stroke appear (high body temperature, confusion, seizures)
  • Persistent vomiting or inability to drink fluids
  • Rapid or irregular heartbeat

üßæ Adult First Aid: Helping Someone During Hyperventilation / Panic Attack

If an adult experiences hyperventilation or a panic attack, stay calm and reassure them. Encourage slow, deep breaths to help reduce rapid breathing. Create a quiet, safe space and stay with them until symptoms ease. Avoid criticizing or telling them to "calm down." If symptoms persist, worsen, or if it is their first episode, seek medical advice.

‚ö†Ô∏è Recognizing Hyperventilation / Panic Attack

  • Chest tightness or discomfort
  • Dizziness or light-headedness
  • Numbness or tingling in hands, feet, or around the mouth
  • Feeling of choking or suffocation
  • Palpitations or rapid heartbeat
  • Fear of losing control, going crazy, or dying

  • Encourage slow, deep breathing ‚Äì in through the nose, out through the mouth
  • Help them focus on breathing by counting or using a breathing exercise
  • Guide them to sit down in a comfortable position
  • Remove or reduce environmental triggers if possible (noise, crowds)
  • Do not use a paper bag to breathe into, as this is not recommended

üßç‚Äç‚ôÇÔ∏è Aftercare and Support

  • Stay with the person until they feel better and breathing normalizes
  • Encourage rest and hydration once calm
  • Help them identify triggers and coping strategies for future attacks
  • Offer to assist in contacting a healthcare professional if needed
  • Remain patient and avoid judgment or pressure

üîÑ Ongoing Management

  • Advise on professional counseling or therapy if attacks are frequent
  • Maintain healthy lifestyle habits including sleep and exercise
  • Educate about breathing exercises to manage symptoms

üÜò When to Seek Emergency Help

  • Person loses consciousness or becomes unresponsive
  • Chest pain or suspected heart attack symptoms
  • Difficulty speaking or severe weakness
  • Seizures occur
  • Symptoms worsen despite breathing techniques
  • First-time episode with severe symptoms

üßæ Adult First Aid: Caring for Someone with Dehydration

If an adult shows signs of dehydration, act promptly by encouraging fluid intake and resting in a cool place. Mild dehydration can often be treated at home, but severe dehydration requires immediate medical attention. Watch for warning signs and seek emergency help if symptoms worsen or the person is unable to keep fluids down.

‚ö†Ô∏è Recognizing Dehydration

  • Dry mouth and sticky tongue
  • Dark yellow, strong-smelling urine or infrequent urination
  • Dizziness or light-headedness, especially when standing
  • Fatigue or weakness
  • Headache or confusion
  • Rapid heartbeat or breathing
  • Sunken eyes or dry skin that doesn‚Äôt bounce back when pinched

üöë What to Do for Dehydration

  • Move the person to a cool, shaded area
  • Encourage slow sips of water or oral rehydration solutions
  • Avoid giving sugary, caffeinated, or alcoholic drinks
  • Rest and avoid physical exertion
  • Loosen or remove tight clothing
  • If vomiting or diarrhea is present, provide fluids frequently in small amounts

üßç‚Äç‚ôÇÔ∏è Monitoring and Aftercare

  • Monitor for improvement or worsening of symptoms
  • Encourage continued fluid intake over the next 24 hours
  • Watch for signs of heat exhaustion or heat stroke if in hot environments
  • Ensure the person avoids alcohol and caffeine until fully recovered
  • Seek medical advice if symptoms persist or worsen

üîÑ Preventing Future Dehydration

  • Drink plenty of fluids daily, especially in hot weather or during exercise
  • Eat foods with high water content like fruits and vegetables
  • Take breaks in cool or shaded areas when working or exercising outside
  • Wear light, breathable clothing
  • Avoid excessive alcohol and caffeine

üÜò When to Seek Emergency Help

  • Persistent vomiting or diarrhea preventing fluid intake
  • Severe weakness or inability to stand
  • Confusion, irritability, or unconsciousness
  • Rapid heartbeat or breathing
  • Very little or no urination over 8 hours
  • Signs of heat stroke such as high body temperature, headache, or seizures

üßæ Adult First Aid: Helping Someone with Insect Bites

If an adult is bitten or stung by an insect, stay calm and assess the reaction. Clean the area gently, reduce swelling and itching, and monitor for any signs of allergic reaction. Most insect bites cause minor irritation and can be treated at home. Seek medical help immediately if signs of a severe allergic reaction appear.

‚ö†Ô∏è Recognizing Insect Bites and Reactions

  • Redness, swelling, and itching at the bite site
  • Small puncture or sting marks visible on skin
  • Localized pain or burning sensation
  • Blistering or rash around the bite
  • Mild fever or swollen lymph nodes (in some cases)
  • Signs of allergic reaction: hives, widespread rash, swelling of face or throat
  • Difficulty breathing, dizziness, or rapid heartbeat (anaphylaxis)

üöë What to Do for Insect Bites

  • Remove the stinger if visible (use a flat-edged object, avoid squeezing)
  • Clean the area gently with soap and water
  • Apply a cold compress or ice pack wrapped in cloth to reduce swelling
  • Use anti-itch creams or oral antihistamines if needed
  • Avoid scratching to prevent infection
  • Keep the area elevated if swelling is significant

üßç‚Äç‚ôÇÔ∏è Aftercare and Monitoring

  • Watch for signs of infection: increasing redness, warmth, pus
  • Monitor for allergic reactions, especially difficulty breathing or swelling
  • Encourage rest and avoid irritants like tight clothing
  • Seek medical advice if symptoms worsen or do not improve in a few days
  • For multiple bites or bites in sensitive areas, consult a healthcare provider

üîÑ Preventing Insect Bites

  • Wear protective clothing when outdoors, especially in wooded or grassy areas
  • Use insect repellent on exposed skin
  • Avoid scented lotions or perfumes that attract insects
  • Keep living areas clean and free of standing water
  • Check for ticks after outdoor activities and remove promptly

üÜò When to Seek Emergency Help

  • Difficulty breathing, wheezing, or tightness in the throat
  • Swelling of the face, lips, tongue, or throat
  • Loss of consciousness or severe dizziness
  • Rapid heartbeat or chest pain
  • Severe allergic reaction after multiple stings
  • Signs of infection spreading from the bite site
